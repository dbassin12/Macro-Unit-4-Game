Unit 4.1

Financial Assets

Understand the different types of financial assets — stocks, bonds, and more — and how interest rates connect them all.

Stocks

Ownership shares in a company. You're literally buying a piece of the business. Higher risk, higher potential reward. When Apple's stock goes up, every shareholder gets richer — when it crashes, so does your portfolio. No guaranteed return.

Bonds

Loans you give to a government or corporation. They promise to pay you back with interest. Lower risk than stocks. Think of it as being the bank — you lend money and collect interest payments until they return your principal.

Interest Rates & Bond Prices

This is an AP exam favorite: bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions. When rates rise, existing bonds lose value (because new bonds pay more). When rates fall, existing bonds gain value.

FDIC Insurance

The government insures bank deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank. This prevents bank runs. Your savings account is safe even if the bank fails. Stocks and crypto? Not covered.

Unit 4.2

Nominal vs. Real Interest Rates

The Fisher Equation is one of the most tested formulas on the AP exam. Know it cold.

Nominal Rate

The "sticker price" interest rate — what the bank advertises. Does NOT account for inflation. If your savings account says 4.5%, that's the nominal rate.

Real Rate

What your money actually earns after inflation eats away at it. This is what matters for your purchasing power. Real rate = Nominal rate - Inflation.

The Fisher Equation Real Rate = Nominal Rate − Expected Inflation
r = i − π

Positive real rate — your money is actually growing

What if inflation is higher than the nominal rate?

You get a negative real interest rate. Your money literally loses purchasing power even while it's earning interest. This happened in 2021–2022 when inflation hit 9% but savings accounts paid under 1%. Slide the inflation above the nominal rate in the calculator to see it.

Unit 4.3

What Is Money?

Money isn't just cash. It's anything widely accepted as payment. Understand its three functions and how we measure it.

Medium of Exchange

You use it to buy stuff. Without money, you'd need a "double coincidence of wants" — finding someone who has what you want AND wants what you have. Try buying lunch with a poem.

Unit of Account

A common measuring stick for value. Instead of saying "this phone costs 47 chickens," we say it costs $1,200. Everything is priced in the same unit.

Store of Value

You can save it and spend it later. A $20 bill still works next year (inflation aside). Try storing value in bananas — they'll be worthless by Friday.

Unit 4.4

Banking & the Money Multiplier

Banks don't just store your money — they lend most of it out, creating new money in the process. This is how the money supply expands.

Fractional Reserve Banking

Banks only keep a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend the rest. If the reserve ratio is 10%, a $1,000 deposit means $100 in reserves and $900 lent out. That $900 gets deposited elsewhere and the cycle continues.

Excess Reserves

The amount above required reserves that banks CAN lend. More excess reserves = more potential lending = more money creation. In 2026, the Fed's required reserve ratio is 0%, so technically all deposits are excess reserves.

The Money Multiplier Money Multiplier = 1 / Reserve Ratio
Maximum change in money supply = Initial deposit × (1 / RR)

Unit 4.5

The Money Market

Supply and demand for money determines the nominal interest rate. The Fed controls supply; people and businesses drive demand.

Money Demand (MD)

Downward sloping. When interest rates are low, the opportunity cost of holding cash is low, so people hold more money. Three motives: transactions (daily spending), precautionary (emergencies), speculative (waiting for investment opportunities).

Money Supply (MS)

Vertical line — the Fed sets it and it doesn't change with interest rates. The Fed shifts MS through open market operations, the discount rate, and reserve requirements.

Unit 4.6

Monetary Policy

The Federal Reserve's playbook for managing the economy. Know the three tools and the transmission mechanism inside out.

Open Market Operations

The Fed's most used tool. Buying bonds injects money (expansionary). Selling bonds drains money (contractionary). The FOMC decides this at each meeting.

Discount Rate

The interest rate the Fed charges banks for emergency loans. Lowering it = cheaper borrowing for banks = more lending. Raising it = opposite. Rarely used but powerful signal.

Reserve Requirements

The % of deposits banks must hold. Lower RR = more lending. Higher RR = less lending. Currently set at 0% since March 2020. The "nuclear option" — almost never changed.

Scenario Cards: Expansionary or Contractionary?

Click each scenario to reveal what the Fed would do.

📉
Unemployment rises to 8% and GDP is falling
Expansionary policy. The Fed buys bonds, lowers the discount rate. This increases MS → lowers interest rates → increases investment & consumption → increases AD → increases real GDP and employment.
🔥
Inflation surges to 7% with an overheating economy
Contractionary policy. The Fed sells bonds, raises rates. This decreases MS → raises interest rates → decreases investment & consumption → decreases AD → reduces inflationary pressure.
🏠
Housing prices are skyrocketing and consumer spending is excessive
Contractionary. The Fed raises rates to cool borrowing. Higher mortgage rates → fewer home purchases → less construction spending → AD decreases. This was exactly what happened in 2022-2023.
🏭
A recession hits — businesses are laying off workers and cutting investment
Expansionary. The Fed lowers rates to stimulate borrowing. Cheaper loans → more business investment → more hiring → more consumer spending → AD increases. This was the 2020 COVID response.
💵
The dollar is weakening and import prices are rising
Contractionary. Raising interest rates attracts foreign investment → demand for dollars increases → dollar appreciates → imports become cheaper → inflationary pressure eases.
📊
GDP growth stalls at 0.5% with stable 2% inflation
Expansionary. With inflation under control, the Fed has room to cut rates and stimulate growth. Lower rates encourage investment without risking a price spiral.
Unit 4.7

The Loanable Funds Market

Where savers meet borrowers. The real interest rate is determined here, and it drives investment in the economy.

Demand for Loanable Funds (DLF)

Downward sloping. Borrowers (businesses, government) want more loans when real interest rates are low. Think of it as the "demand to borrow."

Supply of Loanable Funds (SLF)

Upward sloping. Savers supply more funds when real interest rates are high (better return on saving). Your savings account and retirement contributions feed this supply.

Crowding Out Effect

When the government borrows heavily (deficit spending), DLF shifts right → real interest rate rises → private investment decreases. Government borrowing "crowds out" private borrowing. This is a key critique of fiscal policy and a common AP exam question. Try clicking "DLF → (Gov't deficit ↑)" above to visualize it.
